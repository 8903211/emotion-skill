# Analysis Framework

Use this reference after records have been selected and normalized.

## Evidence First

Separate:

- **Observed**: messages, timing, reply gaps, repeated words, topic shifts.
- **Inferred**: emotional meaning, pressure, avoidance, attachment pattern.
- **Unknown**: voice/media not transcribed, offline events, missing context.

Do not make personality claims without evidence across multiple turns.

## Basic Metrics

Compute or estimate:

- total messages by sender
- daily message counts
- active days
- first and last message time
- late-night ratio when relevant
- average reply pressure, using qualitative reading when exact reply times are not reliable
- counts of apologies, explanations, accusations, boundary phrases, invitations, refusals, affection, repair attempts

## Seven-Layer Relationship Scan

### 1. Self-Pattern Profile

Ask:

- What kind of person does the user become in this relationship: caretaker, pursuer, defender, performer, rescuer, examiner, avoider, or stabilizer?
- What does the user repeatedly try to earn: confirmation, peace, closeness, forgiveness, respect, being chosen?
- Which parts of the user's life center become smaller when this relationship becomes emotionally active?

Signals:

- repeated explaining or proving
- giving availability before checking personal capacity
- mood tracking the other person's mood
- treating delayed replies, short replies, or accusations as urgent tests

### 2. Relationship Meaning

Ask:

- What function does this relationship serve for the user: companionship, identity confirmation, emotional regulation, rescue fantasy, sexual/romantic tension, practical support, or familiar pain?
- What does the user fear losing if this relationship cools down?
- Is the relationship supporting the user's life, or taking over the user's emotional center?

Signals:

- the user's day becomes organized around the other person's tone
- normal boundaries feel like threats to the relationship
- the relationship provides relief from loneliness but creates new pressure
- the user says they need their own life again

### 3. Exchange Structure

Ask:

- What does each side provide: emotion, action, problem solving, attention, money, companionship, status, logistics?
- What does each side expect back?
- Are they exchanging different currencies?

Signals:

- one side gives solutions while the other wants emotional acknowledgment
- one side gives high availability while the other gives intermittent validation
- repeated "I explained / I proved / I made up for it" cycles

### 4. Power Structure

Ask:

- Who is more afraid of losing the relationship?
- Who sets the topic, pace, and emotional temperature?
- Who apologizes first or repairs more?

Signals:

- one side can accuse and the other immediately self-defends
- one side withholds and the other escalates effort
- closeness depends on one person's mood

### 5. Boundary Structure

Ask:

- Are sleep, work, response speed, privacy, money, and emotional capacity respected?
- Does one person's emotion immediately become the other person's task?

Signals:

- "why didn't you reply" becomes a trial
- tiredness needs repeated proof
- boundaries are treated as rejection

### 6. Stage Structure

Ask:

- Is the relationship in attraction, testing, conflict, repair, dependency, or separation?
- Are expectations ahead of the actual relationship stage?

Signals:

- intimacy language appears before trust is stable
- repair language hides unresolved pressure
- high-frequency contact creates false stability

### 7. Narrative Structure

Ask:

- What role does the user play in the story: rescuer, defendant, examiner, caretaker, pursuer, abandoned one?
- What role does the other person play?
- Is this story repeating across relationships?

Signals:

- repeated self-proof
- "if I say it better, they will stop being upset"
- losing one's own life center

## Conversation Dynamics

Look for:

- topic openers: scene vs interrogation
- pressure points: accusation, silence, mismatch, rejection
- repair moves: apology, humor, explanation, boundary
- escalation moves: long paragraphs, repeated checking, moral framing
- de-escalation moves: naming emotion, slowing down, taking space, changing to a lighter topic

## Chat Atmosphere Analysis

One-question-answer loops happen when the user asks for facts only. Better chats turn facts into a shared scene:

```text
fact entrance + emotional reaction + small scene/role + easy hook
```

Examples of mechanisms:

- name/avatar -> light guess
- work/schedule -> shared complaint or care scene
- food/night -> low-pressure invitation
- refusal -> light withdrawal or next-round rule
- emotion -> name the feeling before solving
- photo/outfit -> concrete observation, not body pressure
- private topic -> ask permission first

## Confidence Labels

Use:

- **High confidence**: repeated pattern across many turns and dates.
- **Medium confidence**: clear in this window but may need more history.
- **Low confidence**: plausible but based on a small sample or missing context.
